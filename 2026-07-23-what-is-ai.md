You've probably heard "AI" used to describe everything from a chatbot to a self-driving car to the recommendation feed on your phone. That's confusing, because those things work in very different ways. This article is about untangling the word itself, so the rest of this blog makes more sense.

## What people usually mean by "AI"

When most people say "AI" today, they mean one specific thing: **large language models**, like the systems behind popular chatbots. These are programs trained on huge amounts of text to predict what word is likely to come next in a sentence.

That's a strange way to build something that can hold a conversation, write code, or explain a historical event — and yet it works surprisingly well. The rest of this article is about why.

**A Brief History**

AI as a field is much older than the current wave of chatbots. Researchers have been building "intelligent" software since the 1950s, including:

- Programs that played chess and checkers
- Systems that could prove simple mathematical theorems
- Rule-based "expert systems" that mimicked human decision-making in narrow fields like medical diagnosis

Those early systems were hand-coded with rules. Someone had to sit down and write out the logic. Modern AI is different: instead of being told the rules, the system learns patterns from data.

## Learning from data, not rules

Here's the shift that makes today's AI possible: instead of a programmer writing "if the patient has a fever and a cough, suggest checking for flu," the system is shown millions of examples and learns the patterns itself.

For language models specifically, the "examples" are text: books, articles, websites, conversations. The system is trained to get better and better at one deceptively simple task — guessing the next word — and along the way, it picks up grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and writing style.

> It's a bit like learning a language by reading an enormous library, rather than by studying a grammar textbook.

## Why this matters for how you use it

Because the model learned from examples rather than fixed rules, it doesn't "know" things the way a database does. It doesn't look anything up. It generates a response based on patterns it absorbed during training. That single fact explains a lot of what AI is good at, and a lot of where it goes wrong — which is exactly what the next two posts on this blog cover.

**What This Means for You**

You don't need to understand the math behind these systems to use them well. But knowing that they're pattern-predictors, not fact-databases, changes how you should treat their answers: useful starting points, not final authorities.

"AI" today mostly means large language models: programs trained to predict text so well that the predictions look like understanding. In the next post, we'll walk through exactly what happens, step by step, when you type a question and hit enter.
