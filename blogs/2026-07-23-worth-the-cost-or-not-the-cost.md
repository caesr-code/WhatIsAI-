AI is getting expensive.

Not necessarily for the people using it, but for the companies building it. At the same time, the number of genuinely powerful models has exploded. GPT-5.6 Sol, Claude Fable 5, Kimi K3, GLM 5.2 and a growing list of others are all competing to be the model you use when the easy stuff isn't enough.

But here's the question: **are the best models actually worth the cost?**

## The current AI model race

There isn't really one "best" model anymore. It depends on what you're doing.

**GPT-5.6 Sol** is built for difficult work across coding, research, science, cybersecurity, computer use and design. It is the sort of model you reach for when you want something complicated done properly, rather than just asking it to rewrite an email.

**Claude Fable 5** is aimed at long, difficult tasks and serious coding and knowledge work. One of the interesting things about it is the idea of giving the model a large project and letting it work through it over a much longer period of time.

**Kimi K3** is probably one of the most interesting models to appear in the race recently. Its huge context window and open model approach make it especially interesting for long documents, coding and agent-style tasks. It also shows that the frontier is no longer just a competition between a few American companies.

**GLM 5.2** is another reminder that the AI race is becoming genuinely global. Models from China and elsewhere are becoming increasingly competitive, while open and open-weight releases are putting more powerful AI into the hands of developers who don't necessarily want to pay for a closed service.

And that's before even getting into all the other models competing in specific areas.

## So what are people actually doing with them?

This is where things get interesting.

People aren't just asking these models to write poems anymore. Developers are using them to build entire websites and applications, debug huge codebases and create tools that would have taken much longer to make manually.

AI agents are also becoming much more useful. Instead of asking a model one question and getting one answer, people can give an AI a larger goal and let it break the work into steps, use tools and keep going.

Researchers are using models to search through large amounts of information and help with scientific and technical work. Creators are using them for writing, images, video and game development. Businesses are automating repetitive tasks that used to require people to spend hours doing the same thing over and over.

The important part is that these models are becoming **tools**, not just chatbots.

## But are they worth paying for?

For most people, probably not every subscription.

A free model is already good enough for casual questions, brainstorming, schoolwork and plenty of everyday tasks. Paying for the absolute strongest model only makes sense if you're actually going to use its extra capabilities.

If you're a developer building software every day, a powerful coding model could easily be worth the subscription. If you're doing serious research or working with enormous amounts of information, a model with a massive context window could save hours. If you're just asking AI what the capital of France is, you probably don't need the most expensive model on Earth.

There is also another option: **open models**.

Models like Kimi K3 and other open or open-weight systems are making the question more complicated. Instead of paying a company every month, developers can sometimes run, customise or build around models themselves. That doesn't make them automatically cheaper or easier, but it gives people more control.

## My verdict

The best AI model isn't necessarily the model with the highest benchmark score.

It's the model that gives you the most useful capability for what you're actually trying to do.

For everyday users, free AI is probably enough. For developers, researchers and people who use AI constantly, paying for a frontier model can be completely worth it. And for people who like experimenting and building their own systems, the rise of open models is probably the most exciting part of the whole competition.

So, **worth the cost?**

Sometimes.

But I think the bigger change is that we're finally reaching the point where the question isn't *"Can AI do this?"*

It's *"Which AI should I use to do this?"*

And that is a much more interesting problem to have.
